Evaluation, properly

The game model is trained on 2023–24 and scored once on a sealed 2025 season. It is measured against real baselines, and — more telling than accuracy — its probabilities are checked for calibration.

Leakage & holdout discipline

The 2025 season is a sealed holdout: exported once, read only by evaluation code, never opened during development or feature iteration. All game features are leakage-safe — built only from each team's prior games (trailing rolling form), so a game is never in its own features.
Why it matters: the easiest way to fake a good sports model is to let the outcome leak into the inputs. Sealing 2025 and using pregame-only features is the difference between a real out-of-sample number and a flattering in-sample one.
Doubleheaders are ordered by (date, game number, game_pk); suspended games that span two dates are de-duplicated; rolling windows require ≥10 prior games so the features are full.
These are the unglamorous guards that keep the holdout honest.

Skill vs. luck, measured

The glossary's "stable skill" vs "mostly luck" labels are not vibes — they are year-over-year self-correlations (min 300 PA both seasons). Higher = more repeatable skill; lower = more luck that won't carry over.

A real effect, drawn honestly

The OPS aging curve (B3)
The fitted quadratic peaks near age 30 — the familiar rise-and-decline. We draw it on a fixed OPS axis (0.600–0.800) rather than zooming in, so the arc looks as shallow as it truly is: individual variation dwarfs the average age effect (r² ≈ 0.001). Zooming the y-axis to fill the frame would turn a small, honest signal into a dramatic-looking one — exactly the trick this site refuses.
This is the same discipline as the effect sizes below: the number is real and worth modeling, but the picture is scaled to its true magnitude, not to whatever makes the story loudest.
An honest chart and an honest confidence interval are the same promise in two forms — show the signal and its size.

How to read these effect sizes

We interpret effects the way Funder & Ozer (2019) argue you should: never as a bare "% of variance explained" (r² systematically understates practical importance), always relative to a benchmark or translated into concrete odds, and mindful that small effects compound at scale.

The game edge compounds
The model calls the winner 55.2% of the time vs. 53.8% for always-pick-home — a ~0.7pp edge. Its Brier improvement over that baseline is 0.002, 95% CI [0, 0.004] (n=2,271).
The CI barely clears zero — honestly, a thin edge. But like aspirin and heart attacks (a per-person effect near r≈.03 that still saved lives at population scale), a fraction of a game per matchup is real across 2,430 games a season. We evaluate against achievable baselines, exactly the paper's doctrine.
The draft, honestly
r = -0.096 means an earlier pick out-produces a later one about 53% of the time — a real tilt, nothing like the 90% intuition. 95% CI [-0.306, 0.103] (n=104).
Three true things: the tilt is real and compounds over hundreds of picks a year; our sample is range-restricted (only players who reached MLB), which attenuates r, so the full-population effect is larger; and n=104 leaves the interval wide — it can't rule out ~0 or ~−0.25. Any one pick is close to a crapshoot; the size of the tilt we can't pin down. Reporting "explains 1% of variance" would hide all of this.
Effects are only meaningful in context
By psychology-calibrated labels, BABIP's year-to-year r = 0.298 would count as "large." We still treat it as mostly luck — because the right comparison is K% at 0.82 within the same measurement system, not survey correlations from another field. That is Funder & Ozer's central point, live: an effect size only means something next to the right benchmark.
House rule: nowhere on this site is an effect reported as r² / "% variance explained" alone — every correlation comes with a benchmark or a concrete-odds translation.
